Red Pet

How can we help pet owners while facing emergency situations?

Overview

More than 60% of Canadian households own at least one pet. However, finding veterinary care, identifying pet symptoms, and the ability to face emergencies are important problems.

Therefore, the goal of this project is to design a digital platform (mobile app) that centralizes vet reviews, provides clinic opening hours, and has reliable resources for pet care.

In this project, we’ve done online research, interviewed pet owners, developed ideas using our persona, created the prototype, and conducted usability testing. Each member of our team leads one of them, and I am leading the ideation process.

Team members - Yue Cao, Yuxin Chen, Ka Yeung Ho, Kewen Wang, and Chi-shiun Yang (me).

Process

We followed the UX design process by IBM. In this case study, there are 5 main sections: Understanding the Problem, Users' Situation and Needs, Ideation, Prototyping, Evaluation & Next Steps. Then finally, my Takeaways.

Understanding the Problem

Before designing our app, we first did online research to find out the situation and problems for pet owners in Toronto. After this, we collect user needs with surveys and interviews. After analyzing the data, we discovered these three pain points:
  1. Limited availability and long waiting times in veterinary care due to a large number of pets and a small number of vets.
  2. Not having plans and enough information while facing emergencies.
  3. Untransparent and high cost when taking pets to clinics.

Users' Situation and Needs

To understand more about our users, we made a persona (Peter with a cat named Milu), his empathy map, and the As-Is scenario. One day, at midnight, Peter discovered that Milu seemed to be feeling unwell, he faced these situations:
  1. Peter cannot identify Milu’s symptoms. When searching on the internet, he was doubtful and confused about the answers he got.
  2. He tried to find opening clinics online, but when he arrived, the clinic was already closed. Peter feels very anxious due to the inaccurate information.
  3. Although Peter called another clinic for an appointment, he had to wait 1.5 hours after they arrived, long waiting times made Peter frustrated and unhappy.

As-Is Scenario

After going through Peter's experience, we developed three main need statements:
  1. Peter needs a way to find reliable information so that he can identify Milu’s emergency and take the best action.
  2. Peter needs a way to decide on which emergency clinic to go to so that Milu can receive emergency care when needed.
  3. Peter needs a way to be distracted or calm during a clinic visit so that he will not experience intense emotions of grief and self-blame.

Ideation

To fulfill our need statements, we started brainstorming and came up with many ideas. Some are absurd such as having a personal pet medication buyer, and others are more useful including an online platform with licensed vets, providing real-time clinic info, and having online vets.

Big Ideas

To decide which ideas to implement, all members of our group vote for their impact and feasibility then draw the prioritization grid based on the voting results.

We decided to adopt all ideas under the "Home Runs", "Big Bets", and "Quick Wins". Although some of them have lower impact or feasibility, we want to keep them at this stage since they are somewhat achievable and beneficial to pet owners.

After deciding which ideas to implement, we made Peter's To-be scenario. The three main changes from the As-Is scenario are as follows:
  1. Instead of feeling unsure about the next steps and checking the pet anxiously, Peter can recall previous information regarding illness symptoms.
  2. While searching for information on the internet, Peter can look at experts' advice and understand the situation better.
  3. Before going to the clinic, Peter can find reviews of clinics online and decide to visit a trusted one and see availability ahead to avoid waiting.

Prototyping

Before creating our prototype, we rethought users’ needs and what could be implemented, then listed our design goals:
  1. Pet owners can create a pet information log to share their pet’s medical history to assist vets in identifying symptoms.
  2. Pet owners can acquire information to compare and determine their pet’s condition without having previous knowledge of specific terminology.
  3. Pet owners can check and see clinic information, such as opening times and wait times, more accurately and centrally than other online resources.

Then, we drew the storyboard of three tasks with app screens and users’ actions:
  1. Onboarding Page - users have to create a new account when using the app for the first time.
  2. Clinic Info - users can search nearby clinics with details and reviews.
  3. Pet Information Log - document daily records and can show them to vets when needed.

Before making our clickable prototype, we did an evaluation by asking our potential users to try to implement the task with our hand-draw prototype. The feedback we got includes that they like the static tab bar at the bottom but having icons with text labels will be better, a back button is necessary when users enter something by mistake, and the “Info” label is unclear about what information it contains. These gave us some new ideas including splitting the “Info” section into “Community“ for a discussion board and “Expert Advice” which contains readings from reliable vets.

Evaluation & Next Steps

To make a better product, we have another set of interviews for our prototype. Each session starts with the introduction of the project, asks the participant to implement the tasks we provided, and answers the questions on the script we wrote. The main findings are as follows:
  1. Participants can navigate the features without guidance, and the “Find Clinic” and “Expert Advice” sections of our app are most useful.
  2. There are too many steps for recording information in the Pet Information Log.
  3. The map pins in “Find Clinic” are not clickable.

Meanwhile, participants also have some questions and new ideas provided to us. For instance, how can the quality of real-time data be maintained? The new ideas include having a symptom checker, a filter for the “Community” page, and a live chat feature.

For the next steps, we would like to get in-depth information about our users' experiences through more interviews and reviews, then come up with new ideas based on the feedback. Through this iterative process, we will be able to make a high-fidelity version that looks good and works well. Finally, we will have a test run to make sure our app meets the platform's requirements and keep making changes based on user feedback and interest. This method makes sure that our final product is not only technically sound but also meets the needs and wants of our users.

Takeaways

Working on a UX group project from research to prototyping is a new and challenging experience for me. I have learned a lot throughout the process and here are three key takeaways:
  1. Collaboration leads to a better design, especially during the ideation and prototyping process. With more perspective, we can have more methods to implement tasks and combine our thoughts also come up with a better idea.
  2. User evaluation and testing do not end after designing the final product. To have a better app that suits users’ needs, we must listen to their feedback and keep updating it.
  3. Don't worry too much about the details. In the beginning, every time I come up with an idea, I will think deeply about whether we can implement it. Leaving them for a moment for inspiration might give a better result.
